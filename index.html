Why Security Matters

Cyber security isn't just an IT problem‚Äîit's everyone's responsibility. Understanding why we protect our organisation helps us all make better decisions every day.

üè¢ What Are We Protecting?

Our organisation holds a wealth of sensitive information that we're trusted to protect. This includes customer data like names, contact details, and payment information. It includes employee information‚Äîyour personal details, salary data, and HR records. It includes business intelligence such as strategies, contracts, financial projections, and intellectual property.

When we talk about "protecting the organisation," we're really talking about protecting people: our customers who trust us with their information, our colleagues whose livelihoods depend on our continued operation, and our partners who rely on our integrity.

‚ö° The Real Impact of Breaches

Security incidents ripple through organisations in ways that aren't always obvious. The immediate costs include incident response, legal fees, regulatory fines, and customer notification. But the longer-term impacts can be even more damaging.

Customers lose trust and take their business elsewhere. Partners become hesitant to share information or collaborate. Employees face stress, overtime, and sometimes job losses during recovery. The organisation's reputation‚Äîbuilt over years‚Äîcan be damaged in days.

Consider this: after a significant breach, companies typically see a 7.5% drop in stock price, and it takes an average of 46 days to recover. For some companies, a major breach has been the beginning of the end.

üéØ Why Attackers Target Organisations Like Ours

Cybercriminals are running businesses‚Äîjust illegal ones. They look for the best return on investment. Large organisations have valuable data and deep pockets. Small organisations often have weaker security. Medium organisations sometimes fall into a dangerous middle ground: valuable enough to target, but without enterprise-level security.

Attackers don't need to specifically target us. Many attacks are automated, scanning the internet for vulnerabilities. If our defences are weaker than our neighbours', we become the easier target‚Äîlike leaving your car unlocked in a car park.

üí° The Human Firewall

Technology defences are essential, but they can only do so much. You are the final line of defence‚Äîand often the most effective one. A suspicious email that you report, a strange request that you question, a process that you follow correctly: these human actions prevent more breaches than any single piece of software.

üìä Security Is Everyone's Job

You might think "I don't have access to anything important" or "I'm not a target." But attackers don't think that way. They're looking for any way in. An assistant's email account can be used to send convincing requests to the CEO. A junior developer's credentials can provide access to production systems. A facilities manager's access badge can open doors to server rooms.

Every person in the organisation has access to something valuable‚Äîeven if it's just the trust that other people place in them. That's why security is everyone's responsibility, regardless of role or seniority.

MODULE 02

Understanding The Threats

Knowing what we're up against helps us recognise and respond to threats effectively. Here are the most common threats facing organisations today‚Äîand how to think about them.

üé£

Phishing & Social Engineering

Attackers manipulate people into revealing information or taking harmful actions. This is the #1 attack vector because it works‚Äîhumans are often easier to exploit than technology.

üîì

Credential Theft & Stuffing

Stolen usernames and passwords from one breach are automatically tested against thousands of other sites. Password reuse turns one breach into many.

üí∞

Ransomware

Malicious software encrypts files and demands payment for the decryption key. Modern ransomware also steals data first, threatening to publish it if you don't pay.

üë§

Insider Threats

Not always malicious‚Äîoften it's accidental data exposure, misconfigured sharing settings, or simply sending sensitive information to the wrong recipient.

üîó

Supply Chain Attacks

Attackers compromise a vendor or service provider to gain access to their customers. Your security is only as strong as your weakest partner.

üì±

Business Email Compromise

Attackers impersonate executives or vendors to trick employees into transferring money or revealing sensitive information. Often highly targeted and researched.

üß† How Attackers Think

Understanding attacker psychology helps you spot their tactics. Attackers exploit fundamental human tendencies: our desire to be helpful, our respect for authority, our fear of negative consequences, and our tendency to act quickly under pressure.

They create scenarios designed to bypass your rational thinking. "Your CEO needs this wire transfer completed before his flight" combines authority, urgency, and the desire to be helpful. "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" triggers fear and urgency. "I'm from IT and I need your password to fix a critical issue" exploits authority and the desire to be helpful.

The common thread? Pressure to act quickly without thinking. Any time you feel rushed to make a security-relevant decision, that's a red flag in itself.

üìà The Evolution of Threats

Past
Obvious Scams
Poor grammar, generic greetings, obviously fake sender addresses. Easy to spot if you were paying attention.
Present
Sophisticated Targeting
Personalised attacks using information from LinkedIn, company websites, and social media. Grammatically correct, contextually relevant, and using legitimate-looking domains.
Emerging
AI-Enhanced Attacks
Deepfake voice calls impersonating executives, AI-written phishing emails that adapt to your responses, and automated reconnaissance at scale.
‚ö†Ô∏è The Attacker's Advantage

Attackers only need to succeed once. Defenders need to succeed every time. This asymmetry means we can't rely on being perfect‚Äîwe need to assume some attacks will get through and have processes to detect and respond quickly. That's why reporting suspicious activity is so important.

üõ°Ô∏è Your Defence Strategy

  • 1

    Pause Before Acting

    If something creates urgency, that's your cue to slow down. Take a breath. Legitimate requests can wait five minutes for verification.

  • 2

    Verify Through Different Channels

    If you get an email asking for something unusual, call the person using a number you already have (not one in the email). If you get a call, hang up and call back using the official company directory.

  • 3

    Question Requests for Secrecy

    Legitimate business rarely requires you to hide your actions from colleagues. Requests for secrecy are designed to prevent you from getting a second opinion.

  • 4

    Never Share Passwords or Codes

    IT will never ask for your password. Period. Neither will Microsoft, Google, your bank, or anyone else legitimate. MFA codes are for you alone.

  • 5

    Report Attempts Even If You Didn't Fall For Them

    Your report helps protect colleagues who might receive the same attack. Security teams use this intelligence to improve defences.

üí° It's Not About Being Paranoid

You don't need to suspect everyone of being an attacker. You just need to verify unusual requests through trusted channels. Think of it like checking your mirrors before changing lanes‚Äîa small habit that prevents accidents.

MODULE 04

Password Security

Let's be honest: we all have too many passwords. The temptation to reuse them is real. But password reuse is one of the biggest security risks you face. Here's how to manage passwords practically.

üí• The Password Reuse Problem

Here's a scenario: You use the same email and password for a shopping site, a forum, and your work account. The shopping site gets breached‚Äîyou might not even hear about it. Attackers now have your email and password, and they automatically try it everywhere: banking sites, email providers, corporate systems.

This isn't theoretical. Billions of credentials have been leaked in breaches over the years. Attackers buy and sell these credentials, and automated tools test them across thousands of sites in minutes. If you've reused a password, you're playing the odds that none of your accounts have been in a breach. Those odds aren't good.

üíº The Practical Solution: Password Managers

A password manager is software that generates, stores, and fills in passwords for you. You only need to remember one strong master password‚Äîthe manager handles everything else.

With a password manager, every account gets a unique, randomly generated password like "7Kx$mP2@nQ9vL&wR". You don't need to remember it because the manager does. When you visit a site, the manager fills in the password automatically.

This solves the reuse problem completely. If one site is breached, your other accounts remain secure because they all have different passwords.

üí° Password Manager Benefits

Unique passwords everywhere: One breach doesn't compromise other accounts.
Stronger passwords: 20+ random characters that you don't need to remember.
Phishing protection: The manager won't fill passwords on fake sites.
Less frustration: No more "forgot password" resets.

üîë For Passwords You Must Remember

Some passwords you need to type manually‚Äîlike your master password or computer login. For these, use a passphrase: a sequence of random words that's long but memorable.

"correct-horse-battery-staple" (25 characters) is much stronger than "Tr0ub4dor&3" (11 characters), and far easier to remember. Length matters more than complexity. Four or five random words, maybe with numbers between them, creates an extremely strong password that you can actually remember.

MODULE 05

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Even the best passwords can be stolen through phishing, breaches, or other attacks. MFA adds a second layer of protection that makes stolen passwords far less useful to attackers.

üîê How MFA Works

Authentication factors fall into three categories: something you know (password), something you have (phone, security key), and something you are (fingerprint, face). MFA requires at least two of these categories.

Even if an attacker steals your password (something you know), they still need your phone (something you have) to log in. This dramatically reduces the value of stolen credentials.

‚ö†Ô∏è MFA Attacks to Know About

Attackers have adapted to MFA. Understanding their techniques helps you stay protected:

  • !

    MFA Fatigue / Push Bombing

    Attackers repeatedly trigger MFA prompts, hoping you'll approve one just to make them stop. Never approve a prompt you didn't initiate. If you're getting unexpected prompts, report it immediately.

  • !

    Real-Time Phishing

    Sophisticated phishing sites can capture your MFA code as you enter it and use it immediately. Always verify you're on the legitimate site before entering credentials.

  • !

    SIM Swapping

    Attackers convince your phone carrier to transfer your number to their SIM, intercepting SMS codes. This is why app-based or hardware MFA is preferred.

üí° Golden Rules for MFA

Never share MFA codes with anyone‚Äînot IT, not your bank, not anyone calling you.
Never approve prompts you didn't initiate‚Äîif you're not actively logging in, deny it.
Report unexpected MFA prompts‚Äîthey mean someone has your password.
Enable MFA on personal accounts too‚Äîespecially email, which is often used for password resets.

Data Protection & Sharing

Being thoughtful about what data we share, with whom, and how, protects our organisation and the people who trust us with their information.

üìä Understanding Data Sensitivity

Not all data requires the same level of protection. Understanding what's sensitive helps you make appropriate decisions about handling and sharing.

üîí

üîë

ü§î The Sharing Decision Framework

Before sharing information, run through these questions:

  • 1

    Does this person need this information?

    Share the minimum necessary. If they need sales figures, do they also need customer names? If they need a summary, do they need the raw data?

  • 2

    Am I certain who I'm sending to?

    Double-check email addresses. Autocomplete can suggest the wrong person. For sensitive data, confirm by phone if you're unsure.

  • 3

    Am I using an appropriate channel?

    Sensitive data shouldn't go via personal email, WhatsApp, or other unapproved channels. Use company-approved systems that have appropriate security.

  • 4

    What will happen to this data afterwards?

    Will the recipient store it? Share it further? Is that appropriate? Consider asking them to delete it after use if it's particularly sensitive.

  • 5

    If this was leaked, what would the impact be?

    If the answer is "significant," add extra precautions: encryption, password-protected files, secure file sharing instead of email attachments.

üåê Sharing with External Parties

Sharing with vendors, partners, or clients requires extra consideration. They may have different security standards, different regulatory requirements, and different data handling practices.

Ask yourself: Can I anonymise or aggregate this data? Can I share a summary instead of raw data? Is there a secure portal instead of email? Has this sharing been approved, or should I check first?

When in doubt, ask your manager or the IT/security team. It's much better to delay by a few hours to get guidance than to create a data protection issue.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Data Sharing Mistakes

CC vs BCC: Using CC instead of BCC exposes everyone's email addresses to each other.
"Reply All" with attachments: Forwarding sensitive attachments to people who don't need them.
Shared link settings: Using "anyone with the link" when "specific people" is more appropriate.
Discussing sensitive topics in public: Calls in coffee shops, screens visible on trains, conversations in lift lobbies.

üóëÔ∏è Data Lifecycle

Data protection isn't just about sharing‚Äîit's about the entire lifecycle. Old data that's no longer needed is still a liability if it's breached. Follow retention policies, delete data when it's no longer required, and don't keep "just in case" copies of sensitive information on your local drive.

Before disposing of devices or storage media, ensure data is properly wiped. Simply deleting files or formatting a drive isn't enough‚Äîdata can often be recovered. Follow your organisation's procedures for secure disposal.

üìã How to Report

1

Stop and Contain

If you clicked something suspicious, disconnect from the network if possible (unplug ethernet or disable WiFi). Don't try to "fix" things yourself‚Äîyou might destroy evidence or make things worse. If it's a lost device, don't assume you'll find it‚Äîreport now.

2

Contact Security Immediately

Email security@yourcompany.com or call the security hotline. Include: What happened? When did it happen? What did you do? What systems or data might be affected? Don't delay‚Äîeven if it's outside business hours.

3

Preserve Evidence

Don't delete suspicious emails‚Äîforward them to security. Screenshot anything unusual before it disappears. Keep devices powered on if possible (unless security advises otherwise). Note down what you remember while it's fresh.

4

Cooperate with Investigation

The security team may have follow-up questions. Stay available and provide any additional information they need. Be honest about what happened‚Äîeven if you made a mistake. This isn't about blame; it's about fixing the problem.

üí° There's No Penalty for Reporting

You will not get in trouble for reporting something that turns out to be nothing. Security teams appreciate vigilance. Every report, even false alarms, helps them understand what's happening across the organisation. The only thing that's discouraged is staying silent when something seems wrong.

"I'm not sure if it's a real threat"

Report it anyway. Let the experts decide. Your gut feeling that something seems off is valuable intelligence.

"I don't want to look paranoid"

Security professionals respect people who report. In this field, healthy scepticism is a virtue.

"It seems too small to matter"

Small incidents can be early signs of larger attacks. Your report might be the piece that completes a bigger puzzle.

"I made a mistake and I'm embarrassed"

Everyone makes mistakes. Reporting immediately lets us respond faster and minimise damage. Hiding mistakes makes things much worse.
