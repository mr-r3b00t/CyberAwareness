MODULE 02

Understanding The Threats

Knowing what we're up against helps us recognise and respond to threats effectively. Here are the most common threats facing organisations today.

üé£

Social Engineering

Attackers manipulate people into revealing information or taking actions. This includes phishing emails, phone calls pretending to be IT support, or impersonating colleagues.

üîì

Credential Theft

Stolen usernames and passwords are bought and sold online. If you reuse passwords, one breach at an unrelated service can compromise your work accounts.

üí∞

Ransomware

Malicious software that encrypts files and demands payment. Often delivered through email attachments or compromised websites. Can halt entire operations.

üë§

Insider Threats

Not always malicious‚Äîsometimes it's accidental data exposure, misconfigured sharing, or simply sending sensitive information to the wrong person.

üéØ How Attackers Think

Attackers look for the path of least resistance. They're not usually targeting you personally‚Äîthey're looking for anyone who might give them access. They exploit urgency, authority, and helpfulness.

A message that says "Your CEO needs this urgently" or "Your account will be suspended" is designed to make you act before you think. Recognising these pressure tactics is your best defence.

‚ö†Ô∏è Remember

Legitimate requests rarely demand immediate action under threat. If something feels urgent and unusual, take a moment to verify through a different channel.

MODULE 03

Password Security & MFA

Your passwords are the keys to our organisation's digital doors. Let's look at practical ways to keep them secure.

üîë The Password Problem

Here's the reality: we all have too many passwords to remember. The temptation to reuse passwords is understandable, but it's one of the biggest security risks you face.

When a service gets breached (and they do, regularly), attackers try those stolen credentials everywhere. If you used the same password for a shopping site and your work email, that shopping breach just became a work problem.

üíº Practical Solutions

  • 1

    Use a Password Manager

    Let software remember your passwords for you. You only need to remember one strong master password. The manager generates unique, complex passwords for each account.

  • 2

    Never Reuse Passwords

    Every account should have its own unique password. With a password manager, this is effortless‚Äîyou don't need to remember them.

  • 3

    Use Passphrases

    For passwords you must remember, use a phrase: "correct-horse-battery-staple" is far stronger and easier to remember than "P@ssw0rd123".

üì± Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds a second layer of protection. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't access your account without your second factor‚Äîusually your phone.

MODULE 04

Data Protection & Sharing

Being mindful about what data we share, with whom, and how, is essential to protecting our organisation and the people who trust us with their information.

üìä Types of Sensitive Data

Not all data is equal. Understanding what requires extra protection helps you make better decisions about handling and sharing.

üë§
Personal Data
Names, addresses, contact details, IDs
üí≥
Financial Data
Payment info, bank details, salaries
üè•

üîí
Confidential Business
Trade secrets, strategies, contracts

ü§î Before You Share, Ask Yourself

  • ?

    Does this person need this information?

    Share only what's necessary for the task at hand. More isn't always better.

  • ?

    Am I using a secure channel?

    Sensitive data shouldn't go via personal email or messaging apps. Use approved company tools.

  • ?

    Have I verified who I'm sending to?

    Double-check email addresses. Autocomplete can send to the wrong "John Smith".

  • ?

    What happens to this data afterwards?

    Consider whether the recipient will store, share, or delete the information.

üåê External Sharing Considerations

When sharing with vendors, partners, or clients, remember they may have different security standards. Consider whether data can be anonymised, summarised, or shared in a more limited form that still meets the business need.

If you're unsure whether sharing is appropriate, ask your manager or the IT team. It's always better to check first than to deal with a breach later.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes

Forwarding emails without checking attachments, sharing links with "anyone with the link" access, and discussing sensitive matters in public spaces or on calls others can overhear. Be mindful of your environment.

MODULE 05

Reporting Security Incidents

Fast reporting can be the difference between a minor incident and a major breach. When in doubt, report it. The IT team would rather investigate a false alarm than miss a real threat.

üö® Report Security Incidents Immediately

üìã What Should You Report?

Report anything that seems suspicious or wrong. You don't need to be certain‚Äîthat's the IT team's job to investigate. Here are examples:

  • !

    Suspicious Emails or Messages

    Unexpected requests for information, urgent demands, strange links or attachments

  • !

    Unusual System Behaviour

    Programs you didn't open, unexpected pop-ups, slow or unresponsive systems

  • !

    Lost or Stolen Devices

    Laptops, phones, USB drives, access cards‚Äîreport immediately even if you might find it

  • !

    Accidental Disclosure

    Sent data to the wrong person, shared something you shouldn't have, found exposed data

  • !

    Suspicious Behaviour

    Someone asking unusual questions, unfamiliar people in secure areas, tailgating

‚è±Ô∏è How to Report

1

Stop and Contain

If it's a suspicious email, don't click anything else. If it's a lost device, try to remember where you last had it. Don't try to "fix" things yourself.

2

Contact IT Security Immediately

Email security@yourcompany.com or call the security hotline. Include what happened, when, and any relevant details.

3

Preserve Evidence

Don't delete suspicious emails‚Äîforward them to security. Screenshot anything unusual. Keep devices powered on if possible.

4

Follow Up

The IT team may have questions. Stay available and provide any additional information they need to investigate.

üí° Remember

There's no penalty for reporting something that turns out to be fine. The IT team appreciates your vigilance. It's far better to report ten false alarms than to miss one real incident.

Not sure if it's a threat?

Report it anyway. Let the experts decide. Your intuition that "something seems off" is valuable.

Worried about looking foolish?

Security professionals respect people who report. Ignoring concerns is what causes breaches.

Think it's too small to matter?

Small incidents can be early signs of larger attacks. Your report might complete a bigger picture.

Made a mistake yourself?

Report it immediately. Honest reporting lets us respond faster. Mistakes happen‚Äîhiding them makes things worse.
